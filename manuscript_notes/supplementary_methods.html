Quantifying ancestry sorting and its predictability across replicate Formica hybrid populations

Supplementary Methods · Formica polyctena × F. aquilonia hybrid populations

We study 20 independent hybrid populations between F. polyctena and F. aquilonia to ask whether parental variation sorts predictably — the same parental allele fixing across populations, as expected if one parental combination is favoured (directional selection; a Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibility, DMI, with unequal fitness) — or in random directions across populations, as expected under drift or under incompatibilities whose alternative combinations are equally fit. Below we define per-locus sorting statistics on ancestry-diagnostic markers, characterise the genomic architecture of differentiation that could confound them, and specify the neutral simulation null under construction. All analyses are reproduced by dev/R/supplementary_analyses.R.

S1. Data

Genotypes are unphased diploid dosages (0/1/2) for 1,114,340 SNPs (minor-allele frequency > 0.05) in 194 individuals: 20 hybrid populations (3–20 diploids each) plus allopatric parental references for F. aquilonia (n = 15) and F. polyctena (n = 15). Each SNP carries a DIEM DiagnosticIndex (DI; higher = more ancestry-diagnostic), a local recombination rate interpolated from the F. rufa linkage map (cM/Mb), and membership in an LD-based effective-marker-locus group (eMLG, "cluster") from a two-stage complexity reduction that defines approximately independent units.

S2. Per-locus sorting statistics

For each locus we orient dosage into an aquilonia-allele frequency using the sign of the parental frequency difference, then classify each hybrid population as near-fixed toward aquilonia (frequency ≥ 1 − fix_th), near-fixed toward polyctena (≤ fix_th), or unsorted (fix_th = 0.1). Writing naqu, npol for the counts of near-fixed populations (nobs with data), the amount of sorting decomposes exactly into a directional and a bidirectional part on a single population-fraction scale:

prop_fixed = |uni_score| + bi_score,  uni_score = (naqu−npol)/nobs,  bi_score = 2·min(naqu,npol)/nobs.

Because all three quantities are fractions of the population panel, a single threshold sort_th classifies every unit — SNP or eMLG — into unsorted, unidirectional-aquilonia, unidirectional-polyctena, or bidirectional, and genome proportions are weighted tallies of that class. This makes SNP-level, eMLG-level and genome-fraction statements directly comparable (fix_th is held constant as a convention; sort_th is the cross-unit knob).

Parallelism test. Predictability of direction is tested per locus among the near-fixed populations as naqu ~ Binomial(nfixed, 0.5), two-sided; rejection indicates a consistent (parallel) direction. With 20 replicate populations this is the per-locus, higher-powered analogue of the cross-population correlation used for three populations by Nouhaud et al. (2022).

Differentiation gate. Near-fixation is evidence of sorting only if the locus was segregating in the founding admixture, i.e. the parents differ; otherwise a near-monomorphic locus is trivially "fixed" in every population and mimics perfect parallelism. Loci are therefore gated on DI (or, equivalently here, on parental differentiation): at DI > −15, 100% of loci have parental allele-frequency difference ≥ 0.5 (medians 0.93, 0.90, 0.89, 0.83 at DI > −15, −20, −25, −30), so DI and parental differentiation agree across the working range.

Result. At sort_th = 0.5 over 16,077 diagnostic (DI > −15) SNPs: 89.9% unsorted, 6.9% aquilonia, 2.7% polyctena, 0.4% bidirectional. The aquilonia>polyctena excess is the directional-sorting signal.

Null choice. A per-locus "pooled" null (setting the expected fixation probability to each locus's own mean ancestry) is circular: its null probability correlates 0.96 with the observed outcome and yields 0 significant loci, versus ~8,000 under the symmetric null. Because the genome-wide mean aquilonia ancestry at diagnostic loci is 0.50, the symmetric (0.5) null is the appropriate, non-circular choice.

S3. Genomic architecture of differentiation (Tier 1)

We asked whether DI is confounded with recombination — the standard driver of "genomic islands." Genome-wide (Spearman): DI vs recombination ρ = −0.03; DI vs within-species π ρ = −0.46; DI vs absolute divergence dxy ρ = +0.13; DI vs cluster size ρ = +0.23.

Two conclusions (Fig. S1). First, DI is essentially orthogonal to recombination (flat across deciles); its dominant correlate is reduced within-species diversity (partly definitional, since a diagnostic marker is near-fixed within each species) rather than elevated absolute divergence. Read through the Cruickshank & Hahn (2014) framework, DI behaves as a relative-differentiation signal, not a barrier-divergence signal. Second, a genuine island signature (elevated FST and dxy, reduced π, very large clusters) is confined to the lowest recombination decile and is largely orthogonal to DI. Statistically, DI ⟂ recombination means the two can be treated as separable predictors of sorting, reducing confounding downstream. Cluster size is a collider of recombination and diversity and is not used as a predictor. (Caveat: π and dxy are computed at MAF-ascertained SNPs, not windowed over all sites, so magnitudes are proxies; the DI⟂recombination result is robust to this.)

S4. Sorting versus recombination at the independent-unit level (Tier 2)

Neutrally, drift per independent unit is recombination-independent; a raw SNP-level relationship between sorting and recombination is inflated by spatial redundancy (linked SNPs in a low-recombination block report a single stochastic event many times). We therefore analysed sorting at the level of LD-pruning representatives (one tag SNP per eMLG cluster), using map-based recombination as the exogenous axis and DI as a separable covariate.

Fraction of units sorted is flat across recombination deciles (Fig. S2A). Standardised regression at the unit level gives a recombination coefficient of essentially zero (prop_fixed ~ recombination + DI: βrecomb = 0.000, p = 0.97) and a small but significant DI effect (βDI = −0.014, p < 10−16), consistent across recombination tertiles (Fig. S2C). Adding cluster size (the collider) spuriously induces a recombination effect, confirming it should be excluded. Thus single-unit sorting is not concentrated in low-recombination regions — consistent with the neutral expectation and in contrast with window-based reports, in which the low-recombination signal is partly the spatial-redundancy artefact that unit-level counting removes.

This is descriptive: "flat with recombination" is consistent with neutrality but does not by itself demonstrate absence of selection, which requires the recombination-matched null (S6). It also concerns single-locus sorting magnitude only; two-locus DMI signatures (correlated sorting of unlinked regions across populations) are tested separately.

S5. Interim conclusions

DI marks low-diversity, ancestry-informative sites spread across the recombination landscape rather than low-recombination regions (S3); single-unit sorting shows no recombination structure (S4); and sorting is overwhelmingly directional, with an aquilonia bias (S2). The descriptive framework thus isolates a predictable directional-sorting signal while showing that the obvious architectural confounders (recombination, DI, cluster size) are either orthogonal or controllable. Causal attribution to selection/DMIs is deferred to (i) a recombination-matched neutral null and (ii) an among-region (two-locus) co-sorting analysis.

S6. LD structure and neutral-null design

To test whether observed sorting exceeds drift, a haplodiploid forward simulation (SLiM) using the empirical recombination map is under construction. A key design decision is how to seed the parental founders. Seeding by independent per-SNP draws reproduces admixture LD (between-species allele-frequency differences) but not within-species haplotype LD. Composite-r² decay in matched panels (n = 15 diploids) shows within-species LD is minor where recombination is high (within/admixture ≈ 15%) but large where recombination is low (≈ 67% overall, ≈ 85% at short range; Fig. S3) — precisely the regions carrying the strongest sorting. This within-species LD is not removed by tightening the DI threshold. Founders will therefore be seeded from real haploid-male haplotypes (males are naturally phased in Hymenoptera), preserving within-species LD in the heritable (gametic) component; demographic parameters will be calibrated to empirical LD decay, heterozygosity and among-population FST, keeping the sorting statistics out of the calibration set.
